# Draft Abstract and Introduction Notes

## Abstract Draft

Large language models are increasingly explored for clinical decision support, yet many existing systems rely on either single-agent reasoning, unconstrained multi-agent debate, or ensemble voting. These designs can improve answer accuracy, but they often provide limited control over how agents exchange evidence, how disagreements trigger verification, and how final outputs can be audited by clinicians. We propose a modular framework for auditable heterogeneous LLM agents based on role-conditioned communication. In the proposed framework, each agent message is conditioned on the sender role, receiver role, evidence requirements, permitted downstream use, risk flags, and escalation rules. The system separates case summarization, evidence retrieval, clinical reasoning, guideline verification, safety critique, uncertainty estimation, and final synthesis into add/remove modules, enabling systematic ablation rather than treating multi-agent collaboration as a monolithic pipeline. We evaluate the framework on public medical question-answering and evidence-grounded clinical decision support tasks, comparing single-agent, RAG-only, homogeneous multi-agent, heterogeneous multi-agent, and role-conditioned communication variants. The evaluation focuses not only on task accuracy, but also on evidence support, hallucination rate, unsafe recommendation rate, abstention behavior, cost, latency, and audit trace completeness. This work positions heterogeneous LLM agents as clinician-in-the-loop support tools rather than autonomous clinical decision makers, emphasizing traceability, modularity, and safety-aware evaluation.

## Introduction Draft

Large language models (LLMs) have shown strong potential in medical question answering, clinical summarization, and decision-support tasks. However, clinical settings impose requirements that go beyond generating a plausible answer. A useful decision-support system must distinguish supported from unsupported claims, expose uncertainty, surface relevant evidence, and allow clinicians to inspect why a recommendation was produced. These requirements are especially important because incorrect or overconfident language model outputs may lead to unsafe interpretation, misplaced trust, or additional review burden.

Recent work has explored multi-agent LLM systems as a way to improve medical reasoning. By assigning agents different roles, enabling debate, or aggregating outputs from multiple models, these systems can sometimes outperform a single LLM. However, multi-agent collaboration is often treated as a fixed conversation pattern or voting procedure. In such settings, agents may exchange free-form messages without explicit constraints on what evidence is required, what type of claim a role is allowed to make, how downstream agents should use a message, or when uncertainty should trigger additional verification. This limits auditability and makes it difficult to understand which part of the agent pipeline actually improves reliability.

We argue that clinical multi-agent systems require a communication layer, not only a role assignment layer. In particular, the way agents communicate should be conditioned on their roles and on the clinical risk of the task. An evidence retrieval agent should not produce diagnostic conclusions; a clinical reasoning agent should separate evidence-supported inferences from hypotheses; a safety critic should be able to block or escalate unsupported recommendations; and a final synthesis agent should preserve the provenance of claims rather than collapse the process into an opaque answer. These role-conditioned constraints make agent collaboration more inspectable and enable modular evaluation.

This paper introduces a role-conditioned communication framework for heterogeneous LLM agents in clinician-in-the-loop decision support. The framework decomposes the system into modular agents for case context summarization, evidence retrieval, clinical reasoning, guideline verification, safety critique, uncertainty estimation, and final synthesis. Each inter-agent message includes structured fields for sender role, receiver role, evidence, allowed downstream action, uncertainty, risk flags, and escalation status. This design allows the system to activate or remove modules depending on uncertainty and task risk, while preserving an audit trace for clinician review.

We evaluate the framework through ablation studies that compare single-agent prompting, retrieval-augmented generation, homogeneous multi-agent collaboration, heterogeneous multi-agent collaboration, and the full role-conditioned communication system. Unlike evaluations that focus only on answer accuracy, our study measures evidence support, hallucination, unsafe recommendation rate, abstention quality, cost, latency, and audit trace completeness. The goal is not to replace clinical judgment, but to study how modular heterogeneous agents can provide more transparent and safer assistance for clinicians reviewing evidence-grounded decision-support outputs.

## Draft Contributions

The paper makes four contributions:

1. We introduce a role-conditioned communication protocol that constrains inter-agent messages by sender role, receiver role, evidence requirement, permitted downstream use, uncertainty, risk flags, and escalation rules.

2. We present a modular heterogeneous LLM agent architecture for clinician-in-the-loop decision support, separating evidence retrieval, clinical reasoning, guideline verification, safety critique, uncertainty estimation, and final synthesis.

3. We design an ablation-based evaluation protocol that tests how adding or removing each module affects accuracy, evidence support, hallucination, unsafe recommendation rate, cost, latency, and audit trace completeness.

4. We provide a safety-oriented framing and audit trace format for studying clinical LLM agents as decision-support tools rather than autonomous diagnostic systems.
